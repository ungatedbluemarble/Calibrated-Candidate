# Reference Preparation Guide
## Skill 04: Interview Prep | Reference File

This guide covers how to prepare your professional references so they can advocate for you effectively. Most candidates treat references as an afterthought. Candidates who prepare their references well gain a material advantage at the final stages of the hiring process.

---

## When References Come Into Play

References are typically requested after a final round interview and before an offer is extended or finalized. Some employers request them earlier, particularly in regulated industries or roles with security clearance requirements. Ask the recruiter when references are likely to be needed so you are not scrambling at the last moment.

Do not wait until you are asked. Identify and brief your references before your first interview. If a hiring process moves quickly and an offer comes faster than expected, you want your references ready to respond within 24 to 48 hours.

---

## Who to Choose

Choose three to five references. More than five is unnecessary and dilutes the signal. Fewer than three raises questions.

**The strongest reference pool covers three dimensions:**

A direct manager who can speak to your day-to-day performance, decision-making, and how you operate under pressure. This is the most credible reference category. If you have more than one strong former manager, prioritize the most recent and the most relevant to the role you are pursuing.

A peer or cross-functional collaborator who can speak to how you work with others, how you handle conflict, and what it is like to be on your team. Hiring managers increasingly value peer references because they reveal interpersonal dynamics that managers sometimes miss.

A senior leader or stakeholder who can speak to your strategic impact and your credibility at higher levels of the organization. This does not need to be your skip-level manager. It can be a client executive, a board member you worked with, or a senior leader from another function who observed your work directly.

**Who to avoid:**

Do not list references who have not agreed to serve. Do not list references you have not spoken to in more than three years without first re-establishing contact. Do not list colleagues who are also job searching: their availability and focus will be inconsistent. Do not list personal references unless explicitly requested: they carry almost no weight in professional hiring.

---

## How to Ask

Ask directly and specifically. Do not say "Would you be willing to be a reference for me?" That is too open-ended and puts the burden on them to define what they are agreeing to.

Say: "I am in the final stages with a company called [Company] for a [Role] position. I would love to list you as a reference. The conversations are usually 15 to 20 minutes by phone. Would you be comfortable speaking to my work on [specific project or area]?"

This framing tells them what they are agreeing to, what they will be asked about, and how much time it will take. It also signals that you are serious and organized, which matters.

Give them an easy out. If someone hesitates, do not push. A reluctant reference is worse than no reference. A warm, credible advocate who is genuinely enthusiastic will always outperform a lukewarm one who agreed out of obligation.

---

## How to Brief Your References

Briefing your references is the step most candidates skip. It is also the step that most directly determines the quality of the advocacy they receive.

After someone agrees to serve as a reference, send them a brief email within 24 hours. Include:

The company name and the full job title you are interviewing for. A two to three sentence summary of what the role involves and why you are pursuing it. The two or three specific strengths or accomplishments you would like them to emphasize if given the opportunity. Any context about the hiring process, such as the stage you are at and what the employer seems to care about most. A note on timing, specifically when they are likely to be contacted and by whom.

Do not write a script for them. References who sound scripted lose credibility immediately. Your goal is to refresh their memory, focus their thinking, and give them enough context to speak specifically and confidently. The words should be theirs.

**What to highlight in your briefing:**

Connect the strengths you want them to emphasize to the specific requirements of the role. If the job description emphasizes cross-functional leadership and stakeholder management, and your reference observed you doing exactly that on a major initiative, name that initiative in your briefing. Give them the anchor. They will use it naturally in the conversation.

---

## What Reference Checkers Actually Ask

Most reference checks follow a predictable structure. Your references should expect questions in these areas:

How long did you work together and in what capacity? What were their primary responsibilities? What are their greatest strengths? Can you give me a specific example of a time they demonstrated [leadership / problem-solving / collaboration]? How do they handle pressure or conflict? What would you say is their area for development? Would you hire them again if you had the opportunity?

The last two questions are the most revealing. Brief your references on how to handle them honestly and constructively. A reference who claims someone has no areas for development sounds dishonest. A reference who names a genuine, minor, and improving area of growth sounds credible and thoughtful.

Advise your references to answer the development question with something real but bounded. For example: "Early in their career they sometimes moved faster than stakeholders were ready for. They have gotten significantly better at managing that pace deliberately. It is no longer a concern." That kind of answer builds credibility rather than undermining it.

---

## Timing and Logistics

When you are told references will be checked, give your references a heads-up by text or email the same day. Tell them who will be calling, from what company, and approximately when. If you do not have the specific name of the reference checker, say so and give them the company name and recruiter name instead.

Make sure you have current contact information for every reference before you list them. A disconnected number or an old email address at a company your reference left two years ago will delay your offer and create a bad impression.

Confirm your references' preferred contact method. Some people prefer email outreach before a phone call. Some will not answer calls from unknown numbers. Pass this preference to the recruiter when you submit your reference list.

---

## After the Reference Checks

Send a thank-you note to each reference after the process is complete, regardless of the outcome. If you receive an offer, tell them and thank them specifically. If you do not receive an offer, tell them that too, thank them for their time, and keep the relationship warm. You will need references again.

Do not assume the process went well because you got the offer. Reference checks occasionally surface information that affects offer terms rather than withdrawing the offer entirely. If your offer comes in lower than expected or with unusual conditions, it is worth asking the recruiter whether the reference check surfaced anything they want to discuss.

---

## Reference List Format

When submitting references, use a clean, consistent format. Do not embed references in your resume. Provide them as a separate document titled "Professional References" with your name at the top.

For each reference include: full name, current title and employer, your relationship to them and the approximate years you worked together, their preferred phone number, and their professional email address.

Example entry:

> Jane Doe
> Vice President of Operations, Meridian Analytics
> Former direct manager, 2019 to 2022
> [phone number]
> [email address]

Three to five entries on one page. No headers beyond the document title. No design elements. Clean and professional.
