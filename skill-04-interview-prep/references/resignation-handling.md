# Resignation Handling Guide
## Skill 04: Interview Prep | Reference File

This guide covers how to resign from your current role cleanly and professionally after accepting a new offer. The way you exit a job is remembered as long as the way you performed in it. A clean resignation protects your reputation, your references, and your professional relationships.

---

## Before You Resign

Do not resign until you have a written offer in hand and have signed it. A verbal offer is not an offer. Offers get rescinded, budgets get frozen, and hiring managers leave companies. Until you have a signed document, you have nothing binding.

Once you have signed and returned the offer, confirm your start date in writing with the new employer before notifying your current one. You need a firm date before you can give notice.

Review your current employment contract before you resign. Check for notice period requirements, non-solicitation clauses, non-compete agreements, and any provisions around intellectual property or client relationships. If you have equity that vests on a specific schedule, understand exactly what you will and will not forfeit based on your last day. If anything is ambiguous, consult an employment attorney before you give notice.

---

## When to Tell Your Manager

Tell your direct manager first, before anyone else. If a peer, a colleague, or a friend at the company finds out before your manager does, it will damage the relationship you most need to exit cleanly.

Do not tell anyone at your current company that you are job searching until after you have resigned. Confidentiality during a job search is not dishonesty. It is professional judgment.

---

## The Resignation Conversation

Request a private meeting with your manager. Do not resign over email if a face-to-face or video conversation is reasonably possible. Email is appropriate only if your manager is remote and a real-time conversation is genuinely impractical, or if the relationship is adversarial enough that you need a written record from the start.

In the meeting, be direct and brief. Do not over-explain. Do not apologize excessively. You are making a professional decision that is within your rights, and you have handled it responsibly by giving notice.

**What to say:**

> "I want to let you know that I have accepted a new position and will be resigning. My last day will be [date]. I want to make this transition as smooth as possible and I am committed to doing everything I can to set the team up well before I leave."

That is enough. You do not owe your manager a detailed explanation of where you are going, why you chose this new opportunity, or what you felt was missing in your current role. If they ask, you can share at your discretion, but nothing you say in this conversation is obligated.

Keep the tone warm and professional regardless of how you feel about the company or the role. Whatever frustration or relief you are carrying, this is not the moment for it.

---

## Notice Period

The professional standard in most industries in the United States is two weeks. Your employment contract may specify a longer period. Honor what is in your contract if it specifies something beyond two weeks.

If your contract requires more than two weeks and your new employer's start date does not accommodate that, you have a few options. You can ask your new employer to push the start date. You can offer your current employer your contractual notice and accept that your start date will shift. Or you can give two weeks and accept any contractual consequences, which in most at-will employment situations are limited. An employment attorney can advise you on exposure if you are concerned.

**Do not offer more notice than you are prepared to give.** Offers to stay for a month or six weeks are well-intentioned but often create awkward situations where the company starts treating you as already gone while you are still showing up. Two weeks is standard for a reason.

**One important exception:** If you are in a senior leadership role, a highly specialized technical role, or a role where your departure creates significant operational risk, offering three to four weeks may be appropriate and may protect relationships that matter to your long-term career. Use your judgment about the role and the relationship.

---

## The Written Resignation Letter

Follow the verbal conversation with a written resignation letter the same day. Keep it to three to five sentences. It is a formal record, not a personal essay.

Include: the date, a statement that you are resigning, your last day of employment, a brief expression of gratitude for the opportunity, and an offer to assist with the transition.

**Example:**

> [Date]
>
> [Manager Name],
>
> I am writing to formally resign from my position as [Title] at [Company], effective [Last Day of Employment].
>
> I am grateful for the opportunities I have had here and for the support of you and the team. I am committed to making this transition as smooth as possible and will do everything I can to wrap up my responsibilities and document my work before my last day.
>
> Thank you for everything.
>
> [Your Name]

Do not use this letter to surface grievances, explain your reasoning, or make requests. Those conversations, if they need to happen, happen separately.

## Handling the Counteroffer

Most candidates who resign from a job they are reasonably good at will receive a counteroffer. Be prepared for it.

The data on counteroffers is consistent: the majority of people who accept a counteroffer leave the company within six to twelve months anyway. The underlying reasons that drove the job search do not disappear because the salary went up. The relationship with your manager is also permanently altered once they know you were willing to leave.

Accepting a counteroffer is not automatically a mistake. There are circumstances where it makes sense: if the new opportunity falls through, if the counteroffer addresses the actual root cause of your dissatisfaction, or if you were primarily using the offer as leverage and the job search was never fully committed. Be honest with yourself about which of these describes your situation.

**If you decide to decline the counteroffer:**

Be gracious and clear. You do not owe a lengthy explanation.

> "I appreciate you going to bat for me, and I want you to know this decision was not easy. I have thought about it carefully and I have decided to move forward with the new opportunity. My commitment to a strong transition remains exactly the same."

Do not say "let me think about it" if you have already decided. Do not negotiate against a counteroffer you have no intention of accepting. That wastes everyone's time and damages trust.

**If you decide to accept the counteroffer:**

Withdraw from the new opportunity immediately and directly. Contact the new employer before your current employer announces anything internally. Be honest: you have decided to remain in your current role. Thank them for the process and the offer. Burning a bridge with a new employer who invested significant time in hiring you is a real cost. Handle it as professionally as possible.

## The Transition Period

Once you have resigned and your last day is set, your job is to leave things better than you found them. This is not about self-sacrifice. It is about professional reputation.

Document everything that only lives in your head: processes, contacts, account details, vendor relationships, recurring tasks, and system access. Create a transition document and share it with your manager by the end of your first week of notice.

Offer to participate in transition conversations with your replacement or your team. Do not overpromise your availability, but do offer reasonable assistance.

Do not slow-walk your exit. Going through the motions for two weeks while mentally checked out is visible and remembered. If you are leaving, leave with the same effort you brought every other day. Your last two weeks are part of your professional record.

**What not to do during the transition period:**

Do not recruit colleagues to join you at the new company while you are still employed. This violates most non-solicitation agreements and is widely considered a significant breach of professional conduct. If colleagues reach out to you after your departure, that is a different matter. Do not initiate it while you are still inside.

Do not take documents, data, client lists, or proprietary materials. Even if you worked on them, they belong to your employer. Taking them creates legal exposure and reputation risk that is not worth it.

Do not disparage the company, your manager, or your colleagues during the transition period or after. You do not know who will cross your path again or in what context. The professional world is smaller than it appears from inside any single organization.

---

## Your Last Day

On your last day, return all equipment, revoke your own access to any systems where you can, and hand off any remaining items cleanly. Send a brief farewell note to colleagues you want to stay in touch with. Keep it warm, brief, and forward-looking.

Connect with people you genuinely want to maintain relationships with on LinkedIn before your last day, while your connection to the company is still clear to them. After you leave, a connection request from an unfamiliar email address is easier to miss.

Leave quietly. No dramatic exit speeches, no airing of grievances, no lengthy farewell emails to the full company. The people who matter know who you are. The rest do not need a summary.
