# Follow-Up Cadence Guide
## Skill 04: Interview Prep | Reference File

This guide covers what to do between interviews and after each stage when you are waiting to hear back. Most candidates either follow up too aggressively and create a negative impression, or go silent and lose momentum. The guidance here is calibrated to keep you visible without becoming a liability.

---

## The Core Principle

Follow-up is not about reminding the employer you exist. It is about demonstrating the same professionalism, judgment, and follow-through in the hiring process that you would bring to the job. Every touchpoint is a data point about how you operate.

A candidate who sends a thoughtful thank-you note within 24 hours, follows up once at a reasonable interval, and then waits patiently demonstrates exactly the kind of professional discipline most employers want to hire. A candidate who sends multiple messages, escalates to LinkedIn, and asks the recruiter for updates every three days demonstrates the opposite.

---

## Stage-by-Stage Cadence

### After the Recruiter Screen

Send a thank-you note within 24 hours. See the thank-you note guidance in the main Skill 04 document for format and content.

If the recruiter told you they would be in touch within a specific timeframe, wait until that date has passed before following up. If no timeframe was given, wait five business days.

**Follow-up if no response after five business days:**

Send a single brief email to the recruiter. Keep it to two to three sentences. Reference the date of your conversation, restate your interest, and ask whether there is an update on timing.

> "[First Name], I wanted to follow up on our conversation from [date] about the [Role] position at [Company]. I remain very interested in the opportunity and wanted to check in on the timing for next steps. Happy to provide anything additional that would be helpful."

Do not send a second follow-up after this one. If you hear nothing after an additional five business days, the process has likely moved in a different direction. Mark it as no response in your tracker and move on.

### After the Hiring Manager Interview

Send a thank-you note within 24 hours, personalized to what was discussed. This note is more important than the one after the recruiter screen. The hiring manager is the decision-maker. A specific, well-written note reinforces your fit and your attention to detail.

If the hiring manager or recruiter gave you a specific date by which you would hear back, do not follow up before that date. Wait until the day after the stated date before reaching out.

If no date was given, wait seven business days before following up. The hiring manager pipeline moves slower than the recruiter pipeline. A week is not unusual.

**Follow-up if no response after seven business days:**

Send your follow-up to the recruiter, not directly to the hiring manager, unless the hiring manager specifically invited you to reach out directly. The recruiter owns the process. Going around them creates friction.

> "[First Name], I wanted to check in on the [Role] search. I had a great conversation with [Hiring Manager Name] on [date] and remain very enthusiastic about the opportunity. I understand these processes take time and I am not trying to rush anything. If there is a timeline update or anything you need from me, I am happy to help move things forward."

One follow-up. Then wait.

### After the Panel Interview

Send a separate thank-you note to each panelist within 24 hours if you have their email addresses. If you do not, send one note to the recruiter and ask them to pass along your thanks to the panel. This is less effective but better than nothing.

Panel processes often involve multiple decision-makers coordinating schedules and feedback. They take longer than single-interviewer stages. Wait ten business days before following up if no timeline was given.

**Follow-up if no response after ten business days:**

Follow up with the recruiter only. Keep the same tone as the hiring manager follow-up: interested, patient, professional.

### After the Final Round

Final rounds often move directly to offer conversations or reference checks. The recruiter will usually tell you the next step before you leave the building or end the call.

Send a thank-you note to every executive you met within 24 hours. Each note must be specific. An executive who receives a generic thank-you note will notice.

If the recruiter told you an offer decision would come within a specific number of days, wait until that date passes. Then follow up once with the recruiter.

If no timeline was given, wait five business days. Final round decisions typically move faster than earlier stages because the hiring team has already invested significant time.

**If an offer is imminent:** Do not follow up to accelerate it unless you have a competing offer that creates genuine time pressure. If you do have a competing offer, disclose it honestly to the recruiter and give them a specific date by which you need a decision. Do not manufacture urgency. Recruiters have seen every version of this and can tell.

## When You Have a Competing Offer

This is the one scenario where you can and should accelerate the timeline, because the competing offer creates a real constraint that is fair to communicate.

Call the recruiter. Do not email. A phone call signals that this is a real situation requiring a real conversation.

> "I wanted to let you know that I have received an offer from another company with a decision deadline of [date]. I want to be transparent with you because [Company] is my first choice. If there is any way to accelerate the timeline on your end, I would appreciate it. I do not want to make a decision before I have had the chance to complete my process with you."

Be specific about the date. Do not be vague. Do not say "I have another offer in the works." Say "I have an offer with a deadline of [specific date]." Specificity is what converts this from pressure tactic to legitimate information.

If the company cannot move fast enough, you will need to decide whether to accept the other offer, ask for an extension from the other company, or decline and wait. That is a judgment call only you can make based on your confidence in the first company and the relative strength of the competing offer.

---

## When to Stop Following Up

One follow-up per stage is the standard. Two is the maximum in exceptional circumstances. Three or more signals desperation and poor judgment.

If you have followed up once after a stage and received no response after another five to ten business days, consider the process stalled or closed. Mark it accordingly in your tracker and redirect your energy to other opportunities.

Do not reach out on LinkedIn after receiving no response to email. Do not ask mutual connections to intervene. Do not call the company's main line to ask for the recruiter by name. These actions will not reopen a closed door and will close future doors at the same company.

---

## Following Up After a Rejection

If you receive a rejection, you may send one brief, gracious reply. Keep it to two sentences. Thank them for their time and express that you would welcome the chance to be considered for future roles.

> "[First Name], thank you for letting me know, and for the time invested in getting to know my background. I have a great deal of respect for [Company] and would welcome the chance to be considered for future opportunities when the timing is right."

Do not ask for feedback in this note. If you want feedback, wait two to three weeks and then send a separate, specific request. Something like: "I would welcome any feedback you are willing to share on where my background fell short of what you were looking for. I am committed to improving and I respect your perspective."

Some recruiters will respond. Most will not. Either outcome is acceptable. Do not follow up on the feedback request.

---

## Email Discipline

Every follow-up email should do three things and nothing else: reference the specific conversation or stage, restate interest in one sentence, and ask a clear question or make a clear request.

Keep follow-up emails to three sentences maximum. A long follow-up email signals that you are anxious and not good at editing yourself. Neither is a quality employers want to reward.

Do not attach anything to a follow-up email unless specifically asked. Do not rehash your qualifications. Do not say "I just wanted to check in." Just check in.
